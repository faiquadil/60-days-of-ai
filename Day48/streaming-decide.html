📖 How this was researched

This tool compares standalone US subscriptions to Netflix, Hulu, Disney+, and Max (formerly HBO Max) as of July 2026. Here's how each criterion was built and where sources disagreed.

Price

Pricing reflects each service's standard ad-free tier (the most common apples-to-apples comparison point), cross-checked against each service's ad-supported entry tier. Netflix raised prices on March 26, 2026 (confirmed by CNBC, Variety, and PrimeTimer); Disney+, Hulu, and Max prices were confirmed against multiple independent 2026 pricing guides that agreed within a few dollars of each other. Where guides disagreed by more than $1–2 (which happened for Hulu's ad-supported tier — reports ranged from $9.99 to $11.99), we used the most recently dated source.

Simultaneous streams

Reflects the stream limit on each service's standard ad-free tier (not the top premium tier), since that's the plan most subscribers land on. Disney+ is a genuine outlier here — it offers 4 streams on every tier, while Netflix, Hulu, and Max cap their standard tier at 2 and reserve 4 streams for their pricier top tier.

Video quality (4K/HDR access)

Scored on whether 4K/HDR is available at all, and at what tier. Disney+ includes 4K on every paid tier. Netflix and Max gate 4K behind their most expensive tier (an additional $7–4.50/month over their standard plan). Hulu does not offer a 4K tier at all as a standalone product.

Content library size

This is the criterion with the most estimation involved. No streamer publishes an official up-to-the-day title count. We used Reelgood's published "movies per dollar" and "TV shows per dollar" metrics (from their March 2026 Streaming Value Scorecard, built on their licensed streaming-availability database) and multiplied by each service's ad-free price to back into an estimated total catalog size. ESTIMATE This calculation is ours — Reelgood publishes the per-dollar ratio, not the derived totals shown here, so treat the raw title counts as directional rather than exact.

Kids & family content

EDITORIAL SCORE There's no single published "kids library size" metric that covers all four services consistently, so this score (1–10) is our own synthesis of qualitative reporting: Common Sense Media's family streaming guidance, comparative reviews on parental-control strength, and analysis of how much of each catalog is purpose-built for young children vs. general-audience content that happens to be family-safe. Disney+ and Netflix were repeatedly cited as having the strongest parental controls; Disney+ was consistently cited as having the deepest dedicated young-children's catalog; Max was repeatedly flagged as having the weakest kids-specific offering and least developed parental controls.

Conflicts we had to resolve

  • Netflix's ad-supported tier stream count was reported as both 1 and 2 across sources; we went with 2, matching the majority of 2026 breakdowns.
  • Hulu's ad-supported price ranged from $9.99–$11.99 depending on publish date; we used the most recently dated figure ($11.99).
  • Max's standard tier price appears as both $18.49 and $16.99 in older vs. newer articles; we used $18.49, confirmed by two independently dated 2026 sources.
