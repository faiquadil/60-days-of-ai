# Day 3 - Role Based Prompting

## Question
How can I improve my LinkedIn profile as a fresher?
## Normal Response
Profile Basics

Professional photo — Use a clear, well-lit headshot with a plain background. Profiles with photos get significantly more views.
Custom headline — Don't leave it as just your degree. Write something like: "Computer Science Graduate | Aspiring Data Analyst | Python & SQL"
Vanity URL — Customize your URL (e.g., linkedin.com/in/yourname) to look more professional.

About Section

Write 3–5 lines summarizing who you are, what skills you have, and what kind of roles you're targeting. End with a call to action like "Open to opportunities in…"

Experience & Projects

Even without a job, add internships, freelance work, college projects, and volunteer work.
Use bullet points with action verbs: "Built," "Designed," "Analyzed," etc.
Quantify where possible: "Increased website traffic by 30% during internship"

Education

Add your degree, relevant coursework, GPA (if strong), and any academic achievements or awards.

Skills Section

Add 10–15 relevant skills. Ask classmates, professors, or mentors to endorse you — it adds credibility.

Certifications & Courses

Add any online certifications (Coursera, Google, HubSpot, etc.). These signal initiative and fill gaps in work experience.

Featured Section

Pin your best work — a resume, a project link, a GitHub repo, or a portfolio website.

Engagement Habits

Follow companies you want to work at.
Connect with seniors, professors, and alumni from your college.
Engage with posts in your field (like, comment thoughtfully) — it increases your visibility.
Post about your learning journey, projects, or insights once in a while.

Open to Work

Turn on the #OpenToWork banner or set it visible to recruiters only — it signals availability without looking desperate.

The single biggest differentiator for freshers is showcasing projects — treat each project like a job entry, describing the problem, tools used, and outcome. That's what recruiters look for when there's no work history.Sonnet 4.6 LowClaude
## Founder Persona Response
As a startup founder, I've hired dozens of freshers — and most LinkedIn profiles I see are invisible. Here's how to actually stand out:

1. Headline — Stop Writing "Student at XYZ College"
That's a wasted opportunity. Instead:

"Aspiring Software Engineer | Python · React · Building side projects | Open to roles"

Pack in skills + intent + energy. Make recruiters feel you're already in motion.

2. Profile Photo + Banner

Clean, well-lit headshot (no selfies, no group crops)
Custom banner — use Canva to make one with your skills or a tagline. Most freshers leave this blank. Easy win.


3. About Section — Tell a Story
Don't list your degree again. Write 3–4 lines like:

"I'm a CS grad obsessed with building products people actually use. I've built [X project], contributed to [Y], and I'm actively looking for opportunities in [domain]. Let's connect."

Short. Human. Memorable.

4. Experience — You Have More Than You Think

Internships (even 1-month ones)
Freelance work
College fests / event management roles
Part-time jobs
Research assistant roles

Frame everything with impact: "Reduced manual work by 40% by automating..." beats "Was responsible for..."

5. Projects Are Your Work Experience
As a fresher, this is your portfolio. For each project:

What problem did it solve?
What tech did you use?
Add a GitHub link or live demo


Recruiters from my team always click projects first for freshers.


6. Skills Section — Be Strategic
Add the top 10–15 skills relevant to your target role. Take LinkedIn skill assessments — the badge genuinely helps with search ranking.

7. Recommendations
Get at least 2–3 recommendations — from professors, internship managers, or project collaborators. Even one good one sets you apart from 90% of freshers.

8. Stay Active — This Is Underrated
Post once a week about:

Something you learned
A project update
Your take on an industry trend

Recruiters notice profiles that are alive. The algorithm rewards it too.

9. Optimize for Search
Use keywords from job descriptions you want in your headline, about section, and experience. LinkedIn is a search engine — treat it like one.

## Developer Persona Response
Profile Basics

Professional photo – clear headshot, good lighting, neutral background
Headline – don't just write "Student at XYZ University"; write something like "Aspiring Software Developer | Python | React | Open to Opportunities"
Custom URL – set it to linkedin.com/in/yourname for a cleaner look

About Section
Write 3–5 lines covering: who you are, your tech skills/interests, what you're looking for, and a call to action (e.g., "Open to entry-level SDE roles or internships").
Experience & Projects (most important for freshers)

Add internships, freelance work, or part-time roles — even non-tech ones show work ethic
If you have no experience, projects are your experience — list them like jobs:

Project name + tech stack used
What problem it solved
Link to GitHub or live demo



Skills & Endorsements

Add 10–15 relevant skills (e.g., Java, SQL, REST APIs, Git, Data Structures)
Ask classmates/professors to endorse you — it adds credibility

Education

List your degree with relevant coursework, CGPA (if above 7.5/10), and any academic achievements

Certifications
Add any online certs — freeCodeCamp, Coursera, Google, AWS, HackerRank. These signal initiative.
Open to Work

Turn on the #OpenToWork banner or set it privately so recruiters can find you
Specify roles: Software Engineer, Backend Developer, Full Stack Developer

Activity & Engagement

Post about projects you're building, things you're learning, or bugs you solved
Comment on posts by developers/engineers you follow — visibility matters
Connect with peers, alumni from your college, and recruiters at companies you like

GitHub Link
Always link your GitHub in the Featured or Contact Info section. A well-maintained GitHub is often more impressive than the profile itself for developers.

Quick priority order for maximum impact:

Strong headline
2–3 solid projects with GitHub links
Relevant skills listed
Open to Work enabled
Active posting/commenting
